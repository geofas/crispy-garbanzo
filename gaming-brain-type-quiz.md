# Gaming Brain-Type Quiz

A 20-question assessment to figure out what kinds of games your brain is
naturally wired for — *before* you spend $70 finding out the hard way.

## How this works

Most "what kind of gamer are you" quizzes just ask which games you already
like. That's a tautology. This one asks about how your brain behaves in
situations that have nothing to do with games — what kind of problems you
find satisfying, where your attention drifts, what failure feels like — and
maps those tendencies onto seven gaming archetypes:

1. **Tactician** — deliberate decisions with full information
2. **Reflexive Operator** — fast input, mastery under pressure
3. **Systems Architect** — building machines that run themselves
4. **Explorer** — novelty as the primary reward
5. **Storyweaver** — characters and choices over mechanics
6. **Pattern Solver** — the dopamine of the "aha" moment
7. **Social Player** — the people are the game

Most people are a blend. Your top two scores will tell you more than your
top one.

## Instructions

1. Answer all 20 questions. Pick the option closest to true even when none
   is perfect. If two options feel equally true, pick both — you'll score
   them at half points.
2. Don't peek at the scoring key until you're done.
3. At the end, tally points per archetype using the scoring key. Highest
   one or two = your wiring.

## The Quiz

### 1. You're given 30 minutes alone in a room with a Rubik's Cube you've never solved. What do you do?
- A) Try to figure out the algorithm from scratch — I want to understand *why* it works.
- B) Look up a tutorial and grind the moves until I can do it consistently.
- C) Get bored quickly and look for something else to do.
- D) Try a bit, then start thinking about how I'd design a better puzzle.

### 2. Your favorite kind of movie scene is...
- A) A heist plan unfolding with everything going *just* right.
- B) A fight choreographed so well you watch it twice.
- C) A character making an impossible moral choice.
- D) A wide shot revealing a strange new world.
- E) Two people talking in a way that changes everything.

### 3. When learning a new board game, you...
- A) Read the rules carefully before the first move.
- B) Skim the rules, learn by playing, look up edge cases later.
- C) Want someone to teach me as we play.
- D) Ask "what's the optimal opening?" within three turns.

### 4. You're stuck in traffic. What does your brain do?
- A) Try to figure out the pattern of light timing.
- B) Get lost in a podcast or an imagined conversation.
- C) Drum on the wheel, fidget, get antsy.
- D) Daydream about places I'd rather be.

### 5. Pick the chore you'd least mind doing:
- A) Reorganizing the pantry by category and labeling everything.
- B) Doing a 1000-piece jigsaw puzzle.
- C) Going for a long walk in an unfamiliar neighborhood.
- D) Hosting a dinner party (you cook).
- E) Practicing an instrument or sport for an hour.

### 6. A "good day" of unstructured free time looks like...
- A) Deep focus on one project for six hours straight.
- B) Pickup sports / climbing / dance class.
- C) Wandering a museum, hike, or new city.
- D) A long meandering conversation with someone interesting.
- E) Bouncing between many small things as the mood strikes.

### 7. When you cook, you...
- A) Follow the recipe precisely and weigh ingredients.
- B) Use the recipe as inspiration and improvise.
- C) Want to perfect *one* dish so well it becomes legendary.
- D) Cook with people — the food is secondary.
- E) Build elaborate multi-course things where timing matters.

### 8. You're given $1000 and one free weekend. You'd most enjoy...
- A) Spending it all on a flight to a city you've never been.
- B) Buying a new instrument and learning one song on it.
- C) Hosting an elaborate dinner or event for friends.
- D) Sitting alone optimizing a hobby project or setup.
- E) Going to a great theater performance and dissecting it after.

### 9. You enjoy a problem MORE when...
- A) I have all the information; it's just hard to compute.
- B) I have to gather information before I can even start solving.
- C) The "right answer" depends on values I have to weigh.
- D) Speed matters as much as correctness.
- E) Other people's choices change my optimal move.

### 10. Pick the metaphor that best describes your brain at its best:
- A) A clean spreadsheet.
- B) A camera lens snapping into focus.
- C) A library you can wander.
- D) A locked box with a key you almost have.
- E) A campfire with friends around it.

### 11. When you watch someone else play a game, you...
- A) Mentally critique their choices.
- B) Feel the urge to grab the controller.
- C) Get curious about the world they're in.
- D) Pay attention to the dialogue and characters.
- E) Watch their reactions more than the screen.

### 12. Failure is...
- A) Information. Try again, refine the model.
- B) A reflex tax. Run it back, do it cleaner.
- C) A door I haven't opened yet.
- D) A story beat.
- E) Frustrating; I want to talk it out with someone.

### 13. You're building with LEGO. You...
- A) Follow the instructions to a perfect replica.
- B) Build my own thing without instructions.
- C) Build elaborate scenes with characters and stories.
- D) See how fast I can do the set.
- E) Make a mechanism that *does* something — a crane, a clock, a chain reaction.

### 14. When you read fiction, what hooks you?
- A) A complex world with internal logic.
- B) Characters whose decisions feel real.
- C) A mystery I'm racing the author to solve.
- D) Beautiful sentences I want to reread.
- E) I read mostly nonfiction / how-things-work.

### 15. A song comes on while you're driving. You're most likely to...
- A) Tap out a complicated rhythm on the wheel.
- B) Listen for production details and instrumentation.
- C) Get lost in the lyrics and what they mean.
- D) Skip it if it's not the exact right vibe.
- E) Turn it up and wish someone were here to share this with.

### 16. The kind of "winning" that feels best to you:
- A) Beating an opponent who was actually trying.
- B) A clean solution to a problem nobody else could solve.
- C) Finishing something elaborate I built myself.
- D) Pulling off a moment of skill I couldn't have done a month ago.
- E) Completing a journey or arc.

### 17. When you imagine yourself "in flow," you're...
- A) Reacting to fast input without thinking.
- B) Holding a complex system in your head, all parts visible.
- C) Absorbed in a story or world where time disappears.
- D) Closing in on the answer to something hard.
- E) Synced up with people doing something together.

### 18. A 4-hour escape-room–style experience. The best version is...
- A) Logic puzzles, ciphers, locks.
- B) Physical and hidden — explore the space.
- C) Story-driven, with actors and stakes.
- D) Team-oriented with competing factions.
- E) Timed and hard — beat the clock.

### 19. Pick the setting that excites you most:
- A) A vast unmapped wilderness.
- B) An intricate political court.
- C) A factory floor with humming machines.
- D) An empty arena, a worthy opponent, no excuses.
- E) A cozy room of friends arguing happily.
- F) A locked room with a single envelope on the table.

### 20. How do you feel about repetition / grinding?
- A) Love it — repetition is how mastery happens.
- B) Hate it — once I know the answer, I'm done.
- C) Tolerable if I'm building toward something visible.
- D) Tolerable if I'm with friends.
- E) Tolerable if there's narrative reward.

### Reading your score

After tallying, compute your top two archetypes. The combo matters more
than the absolute numbers. A few common pairings and what they mean:

| Top 2 | Combo flavor |
|---|---|
| T + P | Competitive puzzlers, deckbuilders, tactics with deep theorycrafting |
| T + Y | Long-haul strategy: 4X, grand strategy, real-time-with-pause |
| T + N | Narrative tactics — choices with mechanical teeth |
| R + E | Action-adventure with skill expression (metroidvanias, soulslikes) |
| R + S | Team shooters, fighting games, co-op action |
| Y + E | Open-world survival/builders, colony sims with exploration |
| Y + P | Programming games, optimization puzzles, automation-with-puzzles |
| E + N | Atmospheric/environmental storytelling |
| E + P | Mystery exploration — Outer Wilds territory |
| N + S | Co-op narrative, tabletop-adjacent, social RPGs |
| N + P | Detective games, narrative mysteries |
| S + R | Competitive multiplayer where execution matters |

If your top two are within 2 points of each other, treat both as primary.
If one archetype is 5+ points clear of the rest, lean into it; the others
are flavor.

## Archetype Profiles

### The Tactician

**Cognitive profile.** You enjoy problems where the information is in
front of you and the difficulty is in computing the right move. You're
patient with complexity if the rules are stable. Real-time pressure feels
like noise; thinking time feels like fuel.

**Core mechanics that fit.** Turn-based combat, deckbuilding, asymmetric
matchups, resource curves, draft phases, action-economy puzzles.

**Genres.** Turn-based strategy, tactical RPGs, deckbuilders, 4X,
auto-battlers, real-time-with-pause.

**Try.** *Slay the Spire*, *Into the Breach*, *XCOM 2*, *Inscryption*,
*FTL*, *Civilization VI*, *Tactics Ogre: Reborn*, *Wildermyth*,
*Battle Brothers*, *Cobalt Core*.

**Bounces off.** Twitchy action games (the clock is against you);
purely linear narrative games (no decisions with mechanical weight).

---

### The Reflexive Operator

**Cognitive profile.** Your brain loves the present moment. You get a
clean dopamine hit from execution under pressure, and the satisfaction
compounds: doing-it-better-this-time is the loop you crave.

**Core mechanics that fit.** Tight inputs, timing windows, reactive
decisions, escalating difficulty, mastery curves you can feel.

**Genres.** Action, FPS, fighting, rhythm, character-action, soulslikes,
roguelite action, racing.

**Try.** *DOOM Eternal*, *Hades*, *Sekiro: Shadows Die Twice*,
*Celeste*, *Devil May Cry 5*, *Apex Legends*, *Tetris Effect: Connected*,
*Beat Saber*, *Street Fighter 6*, *Returnal*.

**Bounces off.** Turn-based games (waiting feels punitive); long
cutscenes between fights; sandbox builders without escalating challenge.

---

### The Systems Architect

**Cognitive profile.** You feel deep satisfaction when complexity
emerges from simple rules — and when a machine you built starts running
itself. You'd rather spend an hour optimizing a setup than playing
through prebuilt content.

**Core mechanics that fit.** Production chains, automation, simulation,
emergent behavior, optimization, base-building.

**Genres.** Factory sims, colony sims, city builders, programming games,
management, tycoons.

**Try.** *Factorio*, *Dwarf Fortress*, *RimWorld*, *Cities: Skylines*,
*Satisfactory*, *Oxygen Not Included*, *Opus Magnum*, *Shenzhen I/O*,
*Dyson Sphere Program*, *Workers & Resources: Soviet Republic*.

**Bounces off.** Linear story games where you can't tinker; pure
twitch games; anything where the machine plays itself *for* you.

---

### The Explorer

**Cognitive profile.** Novelty is the primary reward. The map filling
in is the main quest, regardless of what the actual main quest says.
You quietly resent maps that fill themselves in without you.

**Core mechanics that fit.** Open worlds, hidden secrets, environmental
storytelling, non-linear traversal, discovery-gated progression.

**Genres.** Open-world adventure, immersive sims, metroidvanias, walking
sims, survival exploration.

**Try.** *The Legend of Zelda: Breath of the Wild* / *Tears of the Kingdom*,
*Outer Wilds*, *Subnautica*, *Hollow Knight*, *Tunic*, *Elden Ring*,
*Sable*, *Death Stranding*, *No Man's Sky*, *Prey* (2017).

**Bounces off.** On-rails experiences; competitive multiplayer where
the "world" is one map you re-run forever; quest markers that solve
the game for you.

---

### The Storyweaver

**Cognitive profile.** Characters and choices are the game; mechanics
are the delivery system. You'll forgive a lot of jank for one good line
of writing, and you remember games as the protagonists you played.

**Core mechanics that fit.** Branching dialogue, role-playing, stat-driven
choices, consequence systems, character relationships.

**Genres.** Narrative RPGs, visual novels, choice-driven indies, walking
sims, immersive sims with dialogue depth.

**Try.** *Disco Elysium*, *Pentiment*, *Citizen Sleeper*, *Baldur's Gate 3*,
*Mass Effect Legendary Edition*, *Planescape: Torment*, *80 Days*,
*Norco*, *What Remains of Edith Finch*, *Pyre*.

**Bounces off.** Mechanical sandboxes with no characters; competitive
multiplayer with no narrative frame; grindy progression with thin story.

---

### The Pattern Solver

**Cognitive profile.** The "aha" moment is the most addictive feeling
in your brain. You'd rather think for an hour and then act once than
practice an action a hundred times.

**Core mechanics that fit.** Deduction, logic, hidden information made
visible, programming, lateral-thinking puzzles.

**Genres.** Puzzle, mystery, deduction, programming games.

**Try.** *Return of the Obra Dinn*, *The Case of the Golden Idol*,
*Baba Is You*, *The Witness*, *Tunic*, *Outer Wilds*,
*Lorelei and the Laser Eyes*, *The Talos Principle*, *Patrick's Parabox*,
*Chants of Sennaar*.

**Bounces off.** Skill-grinding games where the answer is "execute
better"; pure narrative games without mechanical mysteries to chew on.

---

### The Social Player

**Cognitive profile.** The game is the people. Mechanics are the
excuse. You'll happily play something mediocre with the right group
and bounce off something brilliant alone.

**Core mechanics that fit.** Cooperation, competition, deception,
shared memory-making, asynchronous social loops.

**Genres.** Co-op, party, competitive multiplayer, social deduction,
MMOs.

**Try.** *It Takes Two*, *Helldivers 2*, *Overcooked 2*, *Among Us*,
*Final Fantasy XIV*, *Sea of Thieves*, *Jackbox Party Pack*,
*Deep Rock Galactic*, *Lethal Company*, *Mario Party Superstars*.

**Bounces off.** Deeply lonely single-player experiences; turn-based
solo strategy; long story games you're meant to absorb in silence.

## Caveats

- **This is a wiring quiz, not a destiny quiz.** Plenty of Tacticians
  fall in love with *Hollow Knight*, plenty of Reflexives discover they
  actually love *Disco Elysium*. Use this as a starting hypothesis, not
  a verdict.
- **Mood matters.** The same brain wants different games on a Tuesday
  evening than on a Saturday afternoon. The quiz captures the shape of
  your preferences, not their volume.
- **Genres bleed.** *Outer Wilds* is an Explorer game *and* a Pattern
  Solver game; *Slay the Spire* is Tactician *and* Pattern Solver. If
  your top two archetypes both point at the same game, that's a strong
  buy signal.
- **Anti-patterns are softer than they sound.** "Bounces off" means
  *expect more friction*, not *don't try it*. Some of the best gaming
  experiences come from a genre fighting your wiring just hard enough
  to teach you something new.
